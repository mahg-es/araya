# Whale Curve Analyze

Analyze customer/product profitability using the Whale Curve methodology —
identifying which customers/products create value and which destroy it,
enabling profit-improvement strategies.

## What problem this solves
Traditional P&L hides that 20% of customers often generate 180% of profit
while the bottom 30% destroy value. The Whale Curve visualizes this —
transforming "all customers are profitable" into actionable profit-improvement
decisions.

## When to use
When profitability analysis is needed. When pricing or service-level decisions
are being made. When The Data Professor asks "which customers should we grow
vs. offboard?"

## Input
Customer/product revenue and fully-loaded cost data (from ABC costing model).

## Output
A Whale Curve visualization with cumulative profit ranking, profit tiers,
improvement candidates, and actionable recommendations.

## Steps
1. Gather revenue and fully-loaded cost per customer/product
2. Calculate profit margin per entity: (revenue - cost) / revenue
3. Rank entities from most profitable to least profitable
4. Calculate cumulative profit as a percentage of total profit
5. Plot the Whale Curve: X = cumulative % of entities, Y = cumulative % of profit
6. Identify tiers:
   - **Stars (top 20%):** Generate > 100% of profit; protect and replicate
   - **Core (middle 60%):** Generate ~0-40% of profit; optimize service levels
   - **Dogs (bottom 20%):** Destroy profit; improve, reprice, or offboard
7. Present recommendations with specific dollar impact per entity

## Rules
- Use fully-loaded costs (ABC), not traditional accounting — avoid cost distortion
- Whale Curve almost always shows extreme profit concentration — that's normal
- Stars: protect relationships, study what makes them profitable, replicate
- Dogs: first try profit improvement (reduce service, increase price) then consider offboarding
- Sensitivity analysis: how does the curve change with ±10% cost/revenue?
- Present with data and narrative — numbers alone don't drive decisions
